Standard One: 

The teacher understands the central concepts, tools of inquiry, and structures of the disciplines he or she teaches and can create learning experiences that make these aspects of subject matter meaningful for pupils.



Artifacts:  CMP Unit Plan, “There is No Rose” Z. Randall Stroope

                         Developed while student teaching at Chippewa Falls High School

                Lesson Plan, Men’s Choir, December 6, 2006

                        Developed while student teaching at Chippewa Falls High School


Knowledge of Central Concepts: The CMP (Comprehensive Musicianship through Performance) unit plan I created for the Varsity Women’s Choir at Chippewa Falls High School demonstrates my ability to use knowledge of the central concepts of music to create quality, engaging units and lessons for my students.  The unit plan displays my understanding of the various elements that make up music, including the heart (aesthetic component).  It also addresses numerous National Standards for Music Education in the knowledge, skill, and affective outcomes that I created for my students.  Throughout the unit, students are challenged to explore the central concepts of music within Stroope’s piece and make connections between various parts of the discipline.


Knowledge of Tools of Inquiry: The introductory lesson for “There is No Rose” found in the CMP plan I created for the Varsity Women’s Choir at Chippewa Falls High School challenges the students to use the tools of inquiry of music (reading, listening to, analyzing, describing, and evaluating music) to become familiar with the piece.  The students are asked to analyze the compositional techniques used in relation to the text and determine the aesthetic outcome for the listener.  In this lesson, students begin to think like an expert in the field and use their own skills and intuition to create a connection with a piece of music.


Meaningfulness to Students: The lesson plan I created for Men’s Choir at Chippewa Falls High School challenges the students to analyze a sacred spiritual text and relate it to their lives today.  Students are asked the following questions during the lesson:

  1. •What is the text about?

  2. •How can you relate the story/meaning of the text to an experience in your own life?  Are there any experiences that you have had where you have felt the same excitement and willingness to follow something you believed in?

  3. •What message does the text send that can be transferred to you as an individual in your life today?

The students discuss and journal on the questions listed above during the class period.  By critically thinking about a piece of music and how it relates to their own lives, students are able to connect emotionally with the music they are working on in class, as well as understand how music can enhance the quality of their lives outside the school setting.
